Case Study of Baggage Claims in U.S

Portfolio

Introduction

The Transportation Security Administration paid passengers $3 million over the last five years for claims that airport security screeners broke, lost or stole their luggage or items inside, according to a review of about 50,000 complaints. After investigating to determine if TSA or its agents were responsible, the agency approved or settled with passengers in about 15,000 cases - nearly 1 out of 3 claims filed from 2010 to 2014.

Payments ranged from a few bucks for missing food or medicine to several thousand dollars for jewelry, electronics and other items passengers said were broken or disappeared in TSA's hands. The nation's 30 busiest airports accounted for about two-thirds of all paid claims and about the same share of the government settlement payments.

While John F. Kennedy International Airport in New York topped the list with 857 paid claims and Los Angeles International was next at 791, that's largely a result of the many millions more passengers' TSA screens there.

Strategic Objectives

This analysis examines claim rates across airlines and airports, focusing on key factors such as incident date, airport name, airline name, and claim type. The approach involves calculating the number of claims for each airline and airport and the total number of passengers for each airport quarterly. The primary objectives of this study are as follows:

  • Are there differences in claim rates across airports?
    • Identifying variations in claim rates across airports to determine which locations have higher or lower frequencies of claims.
  • Does the claim rate vary by both airport and quarter?
    • Analyzing claim rates by both airport and quarter to observe any seasonal or quarterly patterns that may affect claims frequency.
  • Do claim rates differ across airlines?
    • Investigating whether specific airlines have higher or lower claim rates, potentially revealing trends related to airline operations or claim types.

    The study employs statistical methods to identify significant differences and uses visual representations, such as bar charts and heatmaps, to illustrate trends clearly.

    This approach aims to provide a comprehensive view of claim rate variations, offering valuable insights for improving claims processes and enhancing overall service quality.

Data in Statistical Analysis

The data for this analysis is sourced from the TSA Claims Database, available on TSA Claims Database and US Airline Flight Routes and Fares 1993-2024

Statistical Analysis Step 1:

The chi-square test was conducted to analyze whether there is a significant difference in the proportion of claims across six major airports: DFW, JFK, LAX, LGA, ORD, and SFO, focusing on data from four airlines (American Airlines, Delta Airlines, Southwest Airlines, United Airlines) for the year 2015.

Hypothesis

Null Hypothesis (H₀): The proportion of claims is the same among airports.


Findings

The test result shows a chi-square statistic of 1455.71, with 5 degrees of freedom, and a p-value of 0.000. Given the significance level (α = 0.05), the p-value is below 0.05, leading to the rejection of the null hypothesis (H₀).


Conclusion

While analyzing the flight tickets, we found that the round-trip tickets were being considered in two separate data entries. Statistical Analysis Step 2:

A chi-square test was performed to investigate differences in the proportion of claims by airport and season (quarter). The goal was to determine if claim rates vary significantly based on both the airport and the quarter.

Hypothesis

Null Hypothesis (H₀): The proportion of claims is the same across all airports and seasons.


Findings

The chi-square test results for each airport are as follows: LAX: Chi-square statistic = 13.21, p-value = 0.004 JFK: Chi-square statistic = 18.14, p-value = 0.0004 DFW: Chi-square statistic = 10.293, p-value = 0.0162 SFO: Chi-square statistic = 3.37, p-value = 0.338 ORD: Chi-square statistic = 7.6, p-value = 0.055 LGA: Chi-square statistic = 2.143, p-value = 0.543


Conclusion

For LAX, JFK, and DFW, the p-values are below 0.05, leading to the rejection of the null hypothesis (H₀). This indicates a significant difference in the claim proportions by quarter for these airports.

For SFO, ORD, and LGA, the p-values are above 0.05, so we do not reject the null hypothesis (H₀). This suggests that claim rates are relatively consistent across quarters for these airports.

Statistical Analysis Step 3:

A chi-square test was conducted to examine the differences in claim proportions among airlines. Since the dataset does not include the total number of passengers for each airline but only provides overall passenger numbers, airline market share data was used to estimate passenger totals for each airline.

Hypothesis

Null Hypothesis (H₀): The proportion of claims is the same among airlines.

Issue and Solution

The dataset does not provide individual passenger counts for each airline. To address this, airline market share data was used to estimate passenger numbers for each airline, enabling a more precise calculation of claim rates.

Issue: The dataset lacks specific passenger counts per airline.

Solution: Airline market share data was applied to approximate the number of passengers for each airline, allowing for a more accurate claim rate analysis.



Findings

The results showed that American Airlines had 297 claims out of an estimated 93,015 passengers, Delta Airlines had 254 claims out of 114,582 passengers, Southwest Airlines had 88 claims out of 141,993 passengers, and United Airlines had 147 claims out of 69,083 passengers.


Conclusion

Reject Null Hypothesis (H₀): The data indicates a significant difference in the proportion of claims among airlines, suggesting that claim rates are not uniform across the airlines analyzed.
